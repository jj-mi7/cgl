Quick Memory Patterns

🟡 Square ending digits

A perfect square NEVER ends in 2, 3, 7, or 8. Use this to instantly eliminate options!

Valid endings: 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, 9
e.g. 482? → ends in 2 → NOT a perfect square ✗

🔵 Square of numbers ending in 5

For any number ending in 5: multiply the tens digit by (tens digit + 1), then append 25.

35² → 3×4 = 12 → append 25 → 1225 ✓
65² → 6×7 = 42 → append 25 → 4225 ✓
75² → 7×8 = 56 → append 25 → 5625 ✓

🟢 Cubes: Digital sum pattern

The digital sum of n³ follows a fixed cycle: 1, 8, 9, 1, 8, 9… (for n = 1,2,3,4…). Use this to verify cube values.

1³=1 → DS=1 | 2³=8 → DS=8
3³=27 → DS=9 | 4³=64 → DS=10→1

🔴 (a+b)² shortcut

Split the number near a round base.
(a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²

98² = (100-2)² = 10000 - 400 + 4 = 9604
52² = (50+2)² = 2500 + 200 + 4 = 2704

🟡 % increase then decrease

If a value increases by a% then decreases by a%, the net change is always a loss:

Net % = –(a²/100)
e.g. +10% then –10% = –1%

🔵 Two successive % changes

Net % when changed by a% then b%:

Net = a + b + (ab/100)
e.g. +20% then +10%
= 20+10+(200/100) = +32%

🟢 X% of Y = Y% of X

This symmetry is extremely handy. Swap when the reverse is easier to compute.

8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = 2
4% of 75 = 75% of 4 = 3

🔴 Percentage point vs %

If something goes from 40% to 50%, it rose by 10 percentage points, but by 25% relatively (10/40 × 100).

CGL loves this distinction in DI questions!

Digital Sum — The MCQ Eliminator

Not for solving — for verifying answers in seconds. Ideal for MCQ-format SSC CGL.

Definition

What is Digital Sum?

Repeatedly add all digits of a number until you get a single digit. That single digit is the digital sum.

Example: 2,347 → 2+3+4+7 = 16 → 1+6 = 7 → Digital Sum = 7
Example: 239 → 2+3+9 = 14 → 1+4 = 5 → Digital Sum = 5
Key Rules

Rule 1 — Skip 9s (and groups adding to 9)

Any digit 9, or any group of digits that sum to 9, can be ignored. This speeds up the process enormously.

239 → skip 9 → 2+3 = 5 ✓ (same result, much faster)
369 → skip 9, skip 3+6 → Digital Sum = 9 (or 0)

Rule 2 — Digital Sum of the Answer = Digital Sum of the Operation

LHS digital sum must equal RHS digital sum. If an option doesn't match, it's wrong.

Q: 13 + 12 = ? → DS of 13+12 = 1+3+1+2 = 7
Options: 24(DS=6), 25(DS=7)✓, 26(DS=8) → Answer = 25

Rule 3 — Works for Multiplication & Division too

DS(A × B) = DS(A) × DS(B) → reduce to single digit.

Q: 24 × 13 = ? → DS(24)=6, DS(13)=4, 6×4=24 → DS=6
Correct answer must have digital sum 6. (Actual = 312, DS=6 ✓)

Rule 4 — Negative Digital Sum: Add 9

If DS comes out negative (in subtraction), add 9 to get the correct digital sum.

DS of (12 - 15) → DS=3, DS=6 → 3–6 = –3 → –3+9 = 6
Check: –3 mod 9 = 6

⚠️ Limitation to know

Digital sum eliminates wrong options but may leave more than one option with the same DS. When two options share the same DS, use standard calculation to pick between them. This still saves significant time by eliminating 2–3 of 4 options instantly.

Calculation Shortcuts

High-frequency tricks for SSC CGL — tested and proven.

Multiply by 11

Write the first and last digits; fill the middle by adding adjacent digits.

36 × 11 → 3_(3+6)_6 → 396
47 × 11 → 4_(4+7)_7 → 4(11)7 → 517

Multiply by 99 / 999

Multiply by 99: N×99 = N×100 – N

45×99 = 4500–45 = 4455
73×999 = 73000–73 = 72927

Divide by 5

Instead of dividing by 5, multiply by 2 and shift decimal left once.

76 ÷ 5 → 76×2=152 → 15.2
340 ÷ 5 → 340×2=680 → 68

Numbers near 100 (Base Method)

For numbers close to 100: find deficit, cross-subtract, multiply deficits.

96×97 → deficits: 4,3
First part: 96–3 = 93
Second part: 4×3 = 12
Answer: 9312

Criss-Cross Multiplication

For 2-digit × 2-digit:
step1: units × units
step2: cross-multiply and add
step3: tens × tens

23 × 12:
3×2=6 | (2×2)+(3×1)=7 | 2×1=2
→ 276

Multiply by 5, 25, 125

×5 = ÷2 then ×10
×25 = ÷4 then ×100
×125 = ÷8 then ×1000

48×25 = 48÷4×100 = 1200
64×125 = 64÷8×1000 = 8000

Square roots: Shortcut

Find the range first (which two round tens the answer lies between), then check the unit's digit of the option against the square's unit digit.

√7744: ends 4 → root ends 2 or 8
7744 is between 80²=6400 and 90²=8100
→ answer is 82 or 88 → try 88²=7744 ✓

Multiply: number × 9ₙ

N × (10ⁿ – 1): subtract 1 from N, then complement each digit of result from 9 to get second half.

654 × 999:
654–1=653 → complement: 346
Answer: 653346

Algebra Identities — Quick Reference

(a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
(a–b)² = a² – 2ab + b²
(a+b)(a–b) = a² – b²
(a+b)³ = a³ + 3a²b + 3ab² + b³
(a–b)³ = a³ – 3a²b + 3ab² – b³
a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²–ab+b²)
a³–b³ = (a–b)(a²+ab+b²)
If a+b+c=0, then a³+b³+c³ = 3abc ← appears often in SSC CGL

SSC CGL Exam Strategy

25 questions in 30 minutes = under 72 seconds per question. Speed is everything.

  • 01
    Fundamentals first, shortcuts second Shortcuts are add-ons — never substitutes. If you don't know the conventional method, a trick will confuse you under pressure. Clear basics first, then layer in shortcuts.
  • 02
    Memorize core tables cold Tables up to 30, squares up to 30, cubes up to 20, fraction–percentage equivalents. These alone save 20–30 seconds per question.
  • 03
    Use Digital Sum for MCQ verification Before computing fully, check which options can be eliminated using digital sum. Reduces 4 choices to 1–2 in under 3 seconds.
  • 04
    Categorize questions on first read In the first 60 seconds, scan all 25 questions. Mark easy (attempt first), medium (attempt second), and skip the clearly time-consuming ones.
  • 05
    Negative marking is –0.5 per wrong answer Attempting a 50-50 guess costs you in expectation. Only guess when you've eliminated at least 2 of 4 options confidently.
  • 06
    Daily 10–15 min calculation drills Practice big-number multiplication, percentage chains, and simplification daily. Speed comes from muscle memory, not just understanding.
  • 07
    Maintain a mistake log Write down every wrong question with the correct method. Review it weekly. SSC repeats question types — your log is a goldmine.
  • 08
    Use substitution for algebra questions When the question asks for the value of an expression given a constraint, try substituting small integers that satisfy the constraint first.
  • 09
    Anchor % calculations at 10% 10% → halve for 5% → double for 20% → add for combinations. Avoid computing from scratch each time.
  • 10
    Solve PYQs (Previous Year Questions) SSC CGL repeats patterns every 2–3 years. Solving 5+ years of PYQs is more valuable than any shortcut book alone.

Word Patterns

Literary & phonetic devices — tested in comprehension, cloze tests, and vocabulary questions.

🔁 Homophones

Words that sound the same but have different spellings and meanings. SSC tests these in spelling/fill-in-the-blank questions.
Examples
affect (verb) / effect (noun) — "The noise affected her. The effect was terrible."
accept / except — "I accept all students except latecomers."
complement / compliment — "Red complements blue. He paid her a compliment."
principal / principle — "The principal has principles."
stationary / stationery — "The bus was stationary. He bought stationery."
elicit / illicit — "Police tried to elicit info about illicit drugs."
capital / capitol — "The capital city has a grand capitol building."
sight / site / cite — "Visit the site; cite the source; what a sight!"
bare / bear — "Bear the bare truth."
flair / flare — "She had a flair for fashion; the flare lit the sky."
⚡ SSC tip: In fill-in-the-blank Qs, homophones appear as trap options. Read the sentence meaning carefully before choosing.

🔤 Alliteration

Repetition of the same initial consonant sound in closely connected words. Appears in comprehension passages and literary extracts.
Examples
"Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers." (P)
"She sells seashells by the seashore." (S)
"Big black bugs bled blue blood." (B)
"The fair breeze blew, the white foam flew." (F & B)
"Bright blossoms bloom beautifully." (B)
"Doubting, dreaming dreams no mortal ever dared to dream before." (D) ← extra example

🔑 HOW TO IDENTIFY: Read the sentence aloud — if you hear the same starting SOUND repeating in 3+ words close together, it's alliteration.
⚠️ Note: It's the SOUND, not the letter. "Knocking on the Night's door" — K and N both qualify as the K-sound starts "knocking".
⚡ In comprehension Qs: Alliteration creates rhythm/emphasis. Identify the repeated sound, not just the letter.

🎵 Rhyme (Rhyming Words)

Words ending in the same sound. Important for identifying patterns in poetry-based comprehension passages.
Common Rhyme Families for SSC
-ight: bright, fight, might, right, night, light, sight
-ound: found, round, sound, bound, ground, wound
-tion: nation, station, relation, ambition, position
-ance/-ence: balance, stance, fence, hence, dense
-ate: fate, mate, late, great, state, inflate, create
-ove: love, dove, above, shove, glove (same sound — ʌv)
Note: "love" rhymes with "above", NOT "stove" — vowel sound matters!
⚡ In PQRS jumbles, rhyme scheme (ABAB, AABB) helps identify correct sentence order in stanzas.

🔀 Homonyms & Homographs

Homonyms: same spelling AND sound, different meaning. Homographs: same spelling, possibly different pronunciation, different meaning.
Examples
bat — cricket bat / flying bat
bank — river bank / financial bank
bear — to bear pain / the animal bear
fair — fair skin / trade fair / fair judgment
bark — tree bark / dog's bark
lead (leed) — to lead a team / lead (led) — a metal
wound (woond) — to wound / wound (wownd) — past tense of wind
tear (teer) — a teardrop / tear (tare) — to rip
⚡ For homographs: stress pattern changes with meaning. "REcord" (noun) vs "reCORD" (verb).

🔊 Onomatopoeia

Words that phonetically imitate the sound they describe. Tested in literary comprehension passages.
Examples
buzz, hiss, crash, bang, splash, murmur, clang
sizzle, clatter, crackle, rustle, gurgle, thud
"The bees buzzed busily." — alliteration + onomatopoeia
"The stream murmured softly." — onomatopoeia

🔁 Assonance & Consonance

Assonance: repetition of vowel sounds in nearby words. Consonance: repetition of consonant sounds (not necessarily at the start).
Examples
Assonance: "The rain in Spain stays mainly in the plain." (long-a sound)
Assonance: "Go slow over the road." (long-o sound)
Consonance: "The lumpy, bumpy camel." (p sound in middle)
Consonance: "He struck a streak of luck." (k sound)

⚖️ Oxymoron, Paradox & Antithesis

Devices that combine contradictory ideas — tested in literary comprehension.
Examples
Oxymoron (2 contradictory words together):
→ deafening silence, living dead, bitter sweet, open secret

Paradox (statement that seems contradictory but holds truth):
→ "I must be cruel to be kind." (Shakespeare)
→ "The more I study, the less I know."

Antithesis (contrasting ideas in parallel structure):
→ "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times."
→ "Give every man thy ear, but few thy voice."

🌟 Simile, Metaphor & Personification

Core literary devices — appear in comprehension passages, cloze tests, and sentence-based Qs.
Examples & How to Identify
SIMILE — comparison using like or as:
→ "He is as brave as a lion."
→ "Life is like a roller coaster."
→ "Her voice was like honey — smooth and sweet." ← extra example
🔑 SPOT IT: Look for the words like / as / as…as / resembles / similar to

METAPHOR — direct comparison, no like/as:
→ "Time is money."
→ "He is a shining star in our team."
→ "The classroom was a zoo after the bell rang." ← extra example
🔑 SPOT IT: No linking word — subject IS the thing. "X is Y" or "X was Y".
⚠️ TRAP: "He ran like a cheetah" = SIMILE (has like). "He was a cheetah on the track" = METAPHOR.

PERSONIFICATION — giving human traits to non-human:
→ "The wind whispered through the trees."
→ "Opportunity knocked at his door."
→ "The sun smiled down on the harvest fields." ← extra example
🔑 SPOT IT: Non-human subject + human verb (whispered, smiled, laughed, wept, danced).
⚡ SSC trick: If "like/as" is present → Simile. If it's a direct IS/WAS comparison → Metaphor. If a thing performs a human action → Personification.

ParadoxA full statement that seems self-contradictory but is true"Less is more"Paradox = full sentence/idea. Oxymoron = two-word contradiction
HyperboleWild exaggeration — impossible to be literal"I've told you a million times"If even slightly possible, it may not be hyperbole
IronySaying the opposite of what is meant (verbal); outcome opposite of expected (situational)"Oh great, another Monday."Irony ≠ coincidence. "It rained on her wedding day" is irony only if she was a weather forecaster
EuphemismMild/indirect word substituted for a harsh one"passed away" for diedDistinguish from metaphor — euphemism is always about social sensitivity
AntithesisTwo contrasting ideas in balanced/parallel structure"Best of times, worst of times"Must be parallel; not just any contrast

🚀 Hyperbole, Irony & Euphemism

Three high-frequency devices in SSC comprehension — often tested by asking you to identify the device OR its purpose.
Hyperbole — deliberate extreme exaggeration
→ "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse."
→ "I've told you a million times to clean your room."
→ "Her smile was so bright, it could light up a stadium." ← extra example
🔑 IDENTIFY: If the statement is physically impossible or wildly unrealistic, it's hyperbole.
PURPOSE: Creates emphasis, humour, dramatic effect.
Irony — saying the opposite of what is meant
Verbal Irony: "Oh wonderful, the flight's been delayed again." (not wonderful at all)
Situational Irony: A fire station burns down.
Dramatic Irony: The audience knows the villain is hiding, the hero does not.
→ "What a beautiful day," she said, watching the storm. ← extra example
🔑 IDENTIFY: Contrast between what is said and what is meant/expected.
Euphemism — a polite/mild word for something harsh
"passed away" → died | "between jobs" → unemployed
"collateral damage" → civilian casualties | "let go" → fired
"economically challenged" → poor | "senior citizen" → old person ← extra example
🔑 IDENTIFY: A soft or indirect phrase that replaces something blunt or uncomfortable.
⚡ SSC tip: Comprehension questions often ask "What device does the author use in line X?" — these three devices appear most often in such questions.

📊 Confusing Word Pairs

SSC loves asking the correct word from a confusing pair. Memorise these distinctions.
High-Frequency Pairs
imply / infer — Speaker implies; listener infers
uninterested / disinterested — uninterested = bored; disinterested = impartial
further / farther — further = more (abstract); farther = more (distance)

Grammar — Rules & Patterns

Error Spotting · Fill in the Blanks · Sentence Improvement · Active/Passive · Direct/Indirect

Spot the Error — Top Rules

Subject-Verb Agreement

Singular subject takes singular verb. Collective nouns (team, committee) are usually singular.

✗ The team are playing well.
✓ The team is playing well.
✗ Each of the students have submitted.
✓ Each of the students has submitted.

Articles — A / An / The

"A" before consonant sounds; "An" before vowel sounds (not vowel letters). Use "the" for specific/unique nouns.

✗ She is a honest woman.
✓ She is an honest woman. (h is silent)
✗ He is an university student.
✓ He is a university student. (u sounds like 'yu')
an MBA, a B.Tech, an MLA, an hour, a one-way street

Tense Consistency

Do not shift tenses within the same clause unless required by context.

✗ He came home and eats food.
✓ He came home and ate food.
Universal truth: always use present simple even in past context.
✓ The teacher said the earth revolves around the sun.

Prepositions — Common Errors

✗ He is married with her. ✓ He is married to her.
✗ She is angry on him. ✓ She is angry with him.
✗ I am confident on success. ✓ I am confident of success.
✗ He jumped in the conclusion. ✓ He jumped to the conclusion.
die of (illness) / die from (injury) / die by (suicide) / die in (accident)

Pronouns — Common Errors

✗ Between you and I, this is wrong.
✓ Between you and me, this is wrong.
✗ Each of them have their book.
✓ Each of them has their book.
Reflexive: myself/yourself used only when subject = object.
✗ Please contact myself. ✓ Please contact me.

Comparison Degrees

✗ He is more cleverer than her.
✓ He is cleverer than her. / He is more clever than her.
✗ Of the two sisters, she is the most beautiful.
✓ Of the two sisters, she is the more beautiful.
"The + comparative, the + comparative" structure:
✓ The harder you work, the better the result.

Modals — Usage

can → ability / permission (informal)
could → polite request / past ability
may → possibility / formal permission
might → less certain possibility
must → strong obligation / deduction
should → advice / mild obligation
ought to → moral obligation
need not → no obligation
dare not → lack of courage
✓ You need not come. / You dare not say that.

25 Power Tips

  • 01
    Attempt English first in Tier-1 It takes least time per correct answer. Solve all 25 English Qs in 18–20 min, leaving more time for Quant and Reasoning.
  • 02
    Synonym trap: "most similar in meaning" Don't pick the first word that looks related. Eliminate options that are only weakly related. The strongest semantic match wins.
  • 03
    Antonym trap: double negatives "Select the antonym of IMPECCABLE" — candidates rush to pick "flawed" but miss "blemished" as the closer option. Always go for the exact opposite in intensity, not just meaning.
  • 04
    Spot the error: always check these first Subject-verb agreement → preposition use → article (a/an/the) → pronoun case → tense shift. These cover 80% of SSC error patterns.
  • 05
    Fill-in-the-blank: use elimination Rule out options that change the tone of the sentence. Then check if the remaining option fits grammatically AND semantically.
  • 06
    Reading Comprehension: skim, don't read Read the 5 questions first. Underline keywords. Then scan the passage for those keywords. Answer directly from text — don't use outside knowledge.
  • 07
    Idiom questions: eliminate by context If you don't know the idiom, look at the sentence context. A positive/negative tone often rules out 2 options. Then guess between 2.
  • 08
    One-word sub: learn root words If you know that -cide = killing, -phobia = fear, -archy = rule, -ology = study, you can solve even unfamiliar options by elimination.
  • 09
    Active/Passive: fully mechanical Memorize the 8 tense patterns cold. These questions are free marks if you know the structure. Never guess — the pattern is fixed.
  • 10
    Narration: watch the tense shift + time words The two most common errors in indirect speech answers: forgetting to shift tense, and forgetting to change "today/here/now" → "that day/there/then".
  • 11
    Cloze Test: read the entire passage first The theme of the passage tells you the register (formal/informal) and helps eliminate options that break the mood. Never solve cloze word by word in isolation.
  • 12
    Para Jumbles: find the opening sentence first The sentence with a proper noun, article "a/an" (not "the"), or general statement is usually sentence 1. The sentence with a pronoun referring to something ("he", "this", "it") is never sentence 1.
  • 13
    Spelling: focus on the double-letter trap SSC consistently tests: accommodation (cc + mm), occurrence (cc + rr), embarrassment (rr + ss), harassment (one r). Learn them as patterns, not spellings.
  • 14
    Daily vocabulary: 10 words minimum Learn in groups of synonyms: ABHOR, DETEST, LOATHE — all mean "to hate". This triples your recall because you learn 3 for the effort of 1.
  • 15
    Don't skip Comprehension — it's 20% of marks In Tier-1, the RC passage yields 5 marks (10 marks in Tier-2). Read carefully; these are the most reliably solved questions if you follow the passage — no memorization required.
  • 16
    Negative marking strategy for English Unlike Quant, English Qs have fewer calculation traps. If you can eliminate 2 of 4 options, attempt it. Your English accuracy should be 85%+ with preparation — much higher than Quant.
  • 17
    Sentence Improvement: the "no improvement" trap Option D is often "No improvement needed." Don't auto-select it from laziness. Read all options first. If none is clearly better, then pick D. Many candidates lose marks here.
  • 18
    Prepositions: there is no shortcut — only exposure Prepositions are idiomatic. "Agree to a proposal / agree with a person / agree on a plan." Build a list of 30–40 common prepositional phrases used in SSC.
  • 19
    Know 100–150 high-frequency idioms cold SSC repeats idioms across years. "A hard nut to crack", "burn the midnight oil", "bite the bullet", "bite the dust", "face the music", "to bell the cat" — these appear repeatedly.
  • 20
    For homophones: always check the meaning, not the sound "The bus was stationary / stationery." Both sound the same when read aloud. Fix your eye on context: "bus" → stationary (not moving). Paper → stationery.
  • 21
    Root-based elimination for new vocabulary Never seen "perspicacious"? Look at the options: A) unclear B) shrewd C) cloudy D) tired. Root "perspic-" (see clearly) → B. Don't panic over unknown words — analyze them.
  • 22
    Revise PYQs topic-wise, not year-wise Take all previous year's synonym questions together, all idiom questions together. This shows you the pattern within each topic far better than solving full papers alone.
  • 23
    Article rule for abbreviations Use "a" or "an" based on the pronunciation of the first letter when spoken aloud. "An MLA" (em-el-a → vowel sound), "a B.Tech student" (bee → consonant sound).
  • 24
    Practice 20 sentences daily for error spotting Consistency beats cramming. Your "grammar instinct" — the ability to feel that something is wrong — develops only through repeated exposure, not theory.
  • 25
    Maintain a Vocabulary Diary When you encounter a new word in a PYQ or mock, write: word → meaning → synonym → antonym → example sentence. Reviewing this weekly will give you 200+ strong words in 2 months.

Number & Letter Series

🔢 Number Series — Pattern Types

A sequence of numbers following a rule. You must find the missing/wrong term. The key is identifying the pattern type.
The 7 Main Patterns
1. ARITHMETIC (constant difference): 3, 7, 11, 15, 19 → +4 each time
2. GEOMETRIC (constant ratio): 2, 6, 18, 54 → ×3 each time
3. SQUARE SERIES: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25 → 1², 2², 3², 4², 5²
4. CUBE SERIES: 1, 8, 27, 64, 125 → 1³, 2³, 3³…
5. DIFFERENCE OF DIFFERENCES: 2, 3, 5, 9, 17 → differences: 1,2,4,8 (×2 each)
6. ALTERNATING: 1, 5, 2, 6, 3, 7 → two interlaced series (+1 and +1)
7. FIBONACCI-TYPE: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13 → each term = sum of previous two
⚡ Step 1: Subtract consecutive terms to find the difference series. Step 2: If difference isn't constant, apply Step 1 again to the differences. Step 3: If still no pattern, check ×/÷ ratios or square/cube relationships.

🔡 Letter Series — Patterns

Letters follow positional or alphabetical patterns. Write A=1, B=2 … Z=26 immediately alongside the given series.
Pattern Types with Examples
POSITION SHIFT: D, G, J, M → +3 positions each (D=4, G=7, J=10, M=13)
REVERSE ALPHABET: Z, Y, X, W → –1 each (Z=26, Y=25…)
SKIP PATTERN: A, C, E, G → every other letter (vowel series)
GROUP PATTERN: ABC, DEF, GHI → groups of 3 consecutive letters
MIXED: A2Z, C4X, E6V → +2 letters, +2 numbers, –2 letters

TRICK — The EJOTY rule memorise: E=5, J=10, O=15, T=20, Y=25
This lets you instantly get positions without counting from A.
⚡ Always write numerical positions below letters. Never try to solve letter series by "feeling" — convert to numbers and treat as a number series.

Analogy & Classification

🔗 Analogy — Relationship Types

A : B :: C : ? — Find the SAME relationship between C and the answer that A has with B.
The 10 Core Relationship Types
1. TOOL → WORKER: Scalpel : Surgeon :: Chisel : Carpenter
2. PART → WHOLE: Chapter : Book :: Scene : Film
3. CAUSE → EFFECT: Virus : Disease :: Fire : Ash
4. WORKER → WORKPLACE: Doctor : Hospital :: Judge : Court
5. PRODUCT → RAW MATERIAL: Bread : Wheat :: Steel : Iron
6. DEGREE: Warm : Hot :: Cool : Cold (intensity increase)
7. ANTONYM PAIR: Brave : Coward :: Honest : Dishonest
8. SYMBOL → QUANTITY: Dozen : 12 :: Score : 20
9. ANIMAL → SOUND: Lion : Roar :: Frog : Croak
10. WORD → STUDY: Entomology : Insects :: Seismology : Earthquakes
⚡ Step 1: Identify relationship type. Step 2: Apply SAME relationship to C. Step 3: Eliminate. Never match surface similarity ("both are animals").

🔎 Classification / Odd One Out

Four items given — three belong to the same group; find the one that doesn't fit. The category can be semantic, positional, mathematical, or linguistic.
Examples by Type
SEMANTIC: Apple, Mango, Potato, Banana → Potato (vegetable, not fruit)
NUMBER-BASED: 16, 25, 36, 50 → 50 (not a perfect square; others are 4², 5², 6²)
LETTER-BASED: BD, FH, JL, MO → MO (gap of 2 skipped letters; M to O skips N — correct, but M=13 is odd; others start on even positions B=2, F=6, J=10)
POSITION-BASED: ABCD, EFGH, IJKL, MNOP → All fit; check if one group has an odd number of letters
CODING-BASED: Odd one out in coded values
⚡ Try multiple categories — alphabetical, numerical, semantic — before picking your answer. SSC often plants a "surface similar" trap: e.g., all four look like countries but one is a capital city.

Coding — Decoding

🔐 Coding–Decoding — All Pattern Types

A word is coded in a certain way — find how another word would be coded. One of the most mechanical topics: once you find the pattern, it's free marks.
Pattern Types
TYPE 1 — LETTER SHIFT (Caesar Cipher): If BOOK = DQQM, each letter shifted +2.
A→C, B→D, C→E … so shift = +2. Apply same shift to decode.

TYPE 2 — REVERSE CODING: If CAT = ZAG (A stays, C→Z, T→G)
Reverse: A=1↔Z=26, B=2↔Y=25… (mirror alphabet)

TYPE 3 — POSITION REPLACEMENT: PENCIL → 123456; CLIP = 4352
Each letter replaced by its position in the original word.

TYPE 4 — WORD CODE (number/symbol): If MANGO = 13141507, each letter = its A-Z position number
M=13, A=1, N=14, G=7, O=15

TYPE 5 — CONDITIONAL CODING: Based on first/last letter type (vowel/consonant), different rules apply. Read the condition table carefully — don't memorise, read per question.
⚡ First move: write the alphabet with positions 1–26 immediately. Then check what happened to 2–3 letters to identify the shift/rule before applying it.

🧭 Direction Sense — 5 Rules

A person walks in various directions — find final direction from start, distance, or displacement. Always draw it on paper.
Rules & Examples
RULE 1: Right turn = 90° clockwise. Left turn = 90° anti-clockwise.
RULE 2: U-turn = 180° turn (you now face the opposite direction).
RULE 3: Displacement = straight-line distance from start to end point.
Use Pythagoras: if 3 km East then 4 km North → displacement = √(9+16) = 5 km NE.
RULE 4: Shadow in morning (sunrise) falls to the WEST. Shadow in evening falls to the EAST.
RULE 5: If facing North → Right = East, Left = West, Behind = South.

EXAMPLE: Start facing North. Walk 5 km North, turn right, walk 3 km, turn right, walk 5 km.
→ End up 3 km East of start, facing South.
⚡ Draw a compass (N/S/E/W cross) at the top of your paper. Mark every step as an arrow. Never track direction mentally alone.

Syllogism & Venn Diagrams

📐 Syllogism — The Venn Diagram Method

Two or more statements → test conclusions. The definitive method: draw all possible Venn diagrams for the statements, then check each conclusion.
Statement Types & What They Mean
"All A are B" → A circle fully inside B circle.
"No A is B" → A and B circles completely separate (no overlap).
"Some A are B" → A and B circles partially overlapping.
"Some A are not B" → part of A circle is outside B circle.

CONCLUSION TEST:
→ If conclusion is true in ALL possible diagrams → "Definitely follows"
→ If true in SOME diagrams only → "Does not definitely follow" (unless "possibly")
→ If false in all diagrams → "Does not follow"

EXAMPLE: All cats are animals. Some animals are dogs.
Conclusion 1: "Some cats are dogs" → Does NOT follow (cats and dogs may not overlap).
Conclusion 2: "Some animals are cats" → FOLLOWS (since all cats are animals).
⚡ The #1 mistake: concluding what is "likely" vs what is "definite". SSC demands logical certainty. "Some" does NOT mean "all", and "No A is B" never means "No B is A" is a conclusion — it already implies that, but students miss it.

🪑 Seating Arrangement — Linear & Circular

Persons seated in a row or circle — decode positions from clues. Most time-consuming topic. Attempt only after all easy Qs are done.
Step-by-Step Method
LINEAR SEATING:
Step 1: Draw boxes for each position (e.g., 6 boxes for 6 persons).
Step 2: Use absolute clues first ("A is at one end").
Step 3: Use relative clues next ("B is 2 positions right of A").
Step 4: Use elimination clues last ("C is not adjacent to D").

CIRCULAR SEATING:
Step 1: Fix ONE person at the top (12 o'clock). This is your reference point.
Step 2: "Facing centre" = left is anti-clockwise, right is clockwise.
Step 3: "Immediate left" of person X = the person sitting anti-clockwise from X.

EXAMPLE CLUE SET (Linear, 5 people A–E):
A is at one end. B is between C and D. E is at the other end. C is between A and B.
→ A – C – B – D – E ✓
⚡ In Tier-1, seating puzzles usually have 4–6 people and 2–4 Qs. Spend max 90 seconds. If you can't crack it in 90s, skip and return at the end.

📊 Ranking Formula

If rank from top = R, rank from bottom = R', total = N:

N = R + R' – 1

Q: Ravi is 8th from top, 5th from bottom. Total?
N = 8 + 5 – 1 = 12 students ✓

Q: 40 students, Priya is 14th from left. Rank from right?
40 – 14 + 1 = 27th ✓

🕐 Clock Angle Formula

Angle between hour and minute hands at H hours M minutes:

θ = |30H – 5.5M|
If >180°, subtract from 360°.

Q: Angle at 3:20?
= |30×3 – 5.5×20| = |90 – 110| = 20° ✓

Coincide: hands meet every 65 5/11 minutes.

📅 Calendar — Odd Days

Odd days = remainder when days divided by 7.

Ordinary year = 1 odd day (365 = 52×7 + 1)
Leap year = 2 odd days (366 = 52×7 + 2)
100 years = 5 odd days
200 years = 3 odd days
300 years = 1 odd day
400 years = 0 odd days ✓
Day code: Sun=0, Mon=1…Sat=6

Two non-verbal types — easy marks if you know the rule.

20 Power Reasoning Tips

  • 01
    Attempt Reasoning second (after English) Easy and medium Qs finish fast. Do series, analogy, odd-one-out first. Do seating/blood relations last.
  • 02
    Number Series: always find the difference series first Subtract consecutive terms. If you get a constant → arithmetic. If differences are multiplying → geometric of differences. 80% of series Qs solved by this alone.
  • 03
    EJOTY is a must-memorize: E=5, J=10, O=15, T=20, Y=25 These anchor points let you get any letter's position in ≤2 steps. No counting from A needed.
  • 04
    Analogy: define the relationship in a SENTENCE before looking at options "Scalpel is the tool of a Surgeon" → now look for "Chisel is the tool of a ___." This prevents falling for visually similar traps.
  • 05
    Odd-one-out: try at least 2 different categories SSC plants traps. If three items seem to share one feature, check if there's also a different grouping of 3 that excludes a different item. Pick the most obvious/specific category.
  • 06
    Coding: write A–Z with 1–26 positions EVERY TIME Takes 20 seconds. Saves you from careless mistakes in the shift. Don't rely on mental alphabets under time pressure.
  • 07
    Blood relations: ALWAYS draw the tree Even for a 1-sentence question. 2-second diagram saves you from misreading "A is the son of B's father" type traps. Most errors here are from skipping the diagram.
  • 08
    Direction sense: draw before reading even the first clue Draw N/S/E/W cross first. Then plot each movement as you read. Never try to "remember" directions mentally.
  • 09
    Syllogism golden rule: test EVERY possible diagram, not just the most obvious one If "Some A are B", three diagrams are possible (partial overlap, A inside B, B inside A). The conclusion must hold in ALL valid diagrams to definitely follow.
  • 10
    Ranking formula: N = R₁ + R₂ – 1 is the only formula you need Memorise it. Covers rank from top + rank from bottom → total, and all variations.
  • 11
    Circular seating: fix one person ALWAYS In a circle of N people, there are (N–1)! arrangements. Fix one as reference. "Right" in circular = clockwise. "Left" = anti-clockwise. This removes ambiguity.
  • 12
    Clock angle formula: |30H – 5.5M| — no exceptions Works for any time. If result exceeds 180°, the reflex angle = 360° – result. Practice 10 different times before the exam.
  • 13
    For Calendar Qs: memorize 100 yrs = 5 odd days, 400 yrs = 0 odd days Most calendar questions are solved by computing odd days for the given year range and mapping to a day code.
  • 14
    Mirror image: the vertical axis never changes up-down The most common student error is flipping both axes. In a mirror image, ONLY left-right flips. Top of letter stays at top.
  • 15
    Non-verbal reasoning: look at ROWS then COLUMNS, never just rows In a 3×3 matrix question, the pattern might run across rows OR down columns OR diagonally. Check all three before selecting.
  • 16
    Venn diagram questions: shade and count circles, don't guess For "only A", "A and B but not C", "all three" — draw three overlapping circles and shade the correct region. Then count from the numbers given.
  • 17
    Input-Output machines: track ONE element through the full operation Don't try to understand the whole step at once. Track what happens to position 1 across all steps, then position 2, etc. The pattern will emerge.
  • 18
    Word formation / dictionary order: write the position numbers, not the word If asked whether DRUM can be formed from THUNDERSTORM — write out T-H-U-N-D-E-R-S-T-O-R-M and mark D, R, U, M. Faster and error-free.
  • 19
    Negative marking strategy for Reasoning Series, analogy, and classification: attempt always (high accuracy). Seating/blood relations: attempt only if you've cracked the setup. Skip incomplete setups — guessing here is –0.5 per wrong attempt.
  • 20
    Revise PYQs topic-wise: SSC recycles Reasoning question patterns heavily The exact numbers change, but the logic pattern repeats. If you solve 50 PYQ blood-relation chains, you'll recognise the next one within seconds.
